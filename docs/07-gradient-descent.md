# Gradient Descent in Living Systems

[← Back to README](../README.md)

---

## Principle

Interaction with systems (body, relationship, work, mind) operates along the **path of least resistance**. Strong priors cannot be overwritten by force.

A system with high resistance = strong priors. Forced overwriting generates massive prediction error → system shuts down or strengthens defenses. Effective change = find where priors are weakest, gradually expand.

---

## Applications

| Domain | Resistance | Strategy |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Massage** | fascial blocks, muscle tension | follow the path of least resistance; what's blocked, you can't force through |
| **Relationship** | defensive mechanisms, blame externalization | grey rock, validation, not confrontation |
| **Work** | politics, resistance to change | find where the system is most permeable |
| **ADHD initiation** | paralysis on boring tasks | defensive apathy = lower perceived importance = lower resistance |
| **Therapy** | traumatic memories | gradual desensitization, not direct confrontation |
| **Learning** | cognitive overload | scaffold from known to unknown, don't dump everything at once |

---

## Mathematical Analogy

Gradient descent in machine learning: optimization through incremental steps in the direction of steepest descent.

- You don't jump to the global minimum — you approach it gradually through local minima
- Step too large = overshooting = system diverges
- Step too small = stuck in local minimum = no progress
- Optimal = adaptive step size based on current landscape

This maps directly to therapeutic and relational work: too much change too fast = system collapses or rebels. Too little = stagnation. The art is calibrating the step size to the system's current tolerance.
